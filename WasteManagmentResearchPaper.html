A Growing Backlash to Trash: A Waste Management Plan in Dhaka, Bangladesh            

Introduction

In its history, the city of Dhaka, Bangladesh, has never seen the amount of economic growth it has experienced in the recent decade. The city has attracted the business of both garment industries and tourists in recent years,1 and its GDP per capita is expected to grow by 4% despite the recent pandemic.2 "When many developed and developing countries are struggling with negative growth due to the Covid-19 pandemic,” boasts Prime Minister of Bangladesh Sheikh Hasina, “our [GDP] growth increased to 5.24%.”3[a] However, as the rate of production of waste in the country increases, and facilities to accommodate such waste remain stagnant or declines, the country faces a substantial risk in the economic sector. This, however, fails to compare to the much larger risk waste pollution servers on the health of local Dhaka residents and the city’s environment.

Currently, Bangladesh produces around 22.4 million tons of waste per year, and in urban areas, it is estimated to reach around 47,064 tons per day by 2025. Of this waste, only around 37% is actually collected.4 In Dhaka, of the 1.65 million tons of waste produced, around 40-50% is collected.5 This amounts to lost revenue in Dhaka’s tourism sector, as well as its fishing sector due to an increase in plastic dumping in nearby water bodies. It also leads to an untapped recycling industry in Dhaka and other Bangladeshi cities such as Chittagong, where the lack of collection of recyclables infringes on the recycling industry’s growth.6 Waste pollution is also a serious concern to the local healthcare of residents. A study done by BMC Public Health reveals that of the 5,562 kg of waste generated each day in health care establishments in Dhaka, 22.6% is hazardous. It also reveals that no health care establishments, besides few private ones, properly manage medical waste to segregate infectious waste.7 Growing pollution of water bodies in the country also leads to water-linked diseases such as cholera, diarrhea, dysentery, and hepatitis.8 Waste pollution also serves as a threat to Bangladesh’s environment. When waste is polluted, it is often either dumped into water bodies or burned, leading to an influx in both water and air pollution.

Plans to address such issues in Bangladesh have been in progress for decades now, yet conditions have only minimally improved. Currently, the Dhaka City Corporation (DCC), with its Northern and Southern branches, administers most of the collection and disposal of waste in Dhaka’s 90 wards, or geo-administrative subdivisions. Due to underfunding and lack of infrastructure, however, the DCC can only collect around 40-50% of produced waste, resulting in residents undergoing other methods of disposal such as illegal dumping in neighborhood streets, water drains, lakes, ponds, etc. On top of that, only 14-17% of the total municipal budget is directed towards solid waste management, and as a result of illegal dumping on streets and drains, the majority of the DCC’s solid waste management budget goes towards road and drain cleaning, as seen in Figure 1.4, 9 This leads to an underfunding in other waste collection sectors such as landfill and vehicle repair. The DCC is also seemingly reluctant to form partnerships with the private sector or NGOs, resulting in a greater strain on the DCC as well as on its budget.9

To devise a plan to minimize waste pollution in Dhaka, Bangladesh, a comprehensive review of current solid waste management plans, as well as the causes of waste pollution in the city, is necessary. This study will conduct a review of such existing plans, and will also investigate the amount of street-side waste bins and public garbage bins in Dhaka. Additionally, this study will recommend a waste management plan intended to limit the amount of waste pollution in Dhaka.  

This study will not focus on the final disposal stage of Dhaka’s waste management program, rather providing particular attention towards primary and secondary collection.

Waste Management in Dhaka

        As mentioned, the two branches of the DCC, Dhaka North City Corporation (DNCC) and Dhaka South City Corporation (DSCC), obtain the responsibility of collecting and disposing of waste in the 145 square kilometers of space it presides over. The DNCC presides over 52 of Dhaka’s wards, and the DSCC presides over the remaining 36.

        Solid waste management in Dhaka is split into three sections: primary collection - the collection of waste from the point where it is produced - secondary collection - the collection of waste from multiple primary collections to a final disposal site via transportation - and final disposal - where waste is either incinerated or embedded into land in a final disposal site, as seen in Figure 3.9,11

Figure 3: Legal Basis of Waste Collection9

Primary Collection

        In Dhaka City, NGO/CBOs and private firms are mainly responsible for primary collection.

        The main method of primary collection in Dhaka buildings is door-to-door collection, where an employee collects waste from dustbins left outside residents’ apartment rooms or flats. Of the 117 high-rise building flats polled in Dhaka, 112 buildings featured a door-to-door collection system, with 111 recruiting local waste collectors for collection, and only 1 recruiting a building employee, as seen in Figure 4.4

Figure 4: Waste collection from household to common collection points4

After the collector collects waste door-to-door, he or she deposits it into a commonly used rickshaw van, as seen in Figure 5. The collector then places the waste in common municipal collection points or street-side waste bins via transportation by rickshaw van. Due to high waste volume and a low quantity of street-side waste bins, as well as rickshaw vans not being able to properly dispose of waste, solid waste is often improperly deposited in the area surrounding the street-side waste bin, as seen in Figure 6, where children employed by informal recycling companies pick the waste for recyclables. Oftentimes, if a street-side waste bin is not provided, waste is dumped in open fields or unused property.4, 13

Secondary Collection

        The secondary collection level is where the DCC obtain the responsibility of the remainder of Dhaka’s solid waste management system.9 

        The DCC’s main mode of secondary collection is via transportation using three types of trucks: Open Truck (OT), Container Carrier (CC), and Trailer Truck (TT). These trucks collect waste from street-side waste bins, properties where waste has been illegally dumped, or through road cleaning. In 2005, these trucks administered waste from 1,071 waste receptacles, or street-side waste bins, of which, 688 units were dustbins, 260 units were 6m3 containers, and 123 were 12m3 containers.9

Final Disposal

The last stage of waste disposal in Dhaka is final disposal, where waste is taken to final disposal sites via transportation following the secondary collection stage. In 2005, a total of three final disposal sites were commissioned. Recently, the Amin Bazar site, which started service in 2007, reached capacity in 2017, leaving only two sites in Dhaka remaining, Matuail and Uttara. In 2005, it was noted that neither site includes covering soil and only Matuail had a surrounding embankment.9, 14

It is obvious the need for a final disposable facility adopting adequate health and safety measurements, however, the scope of this study will not focus on final disposal, and will not recommend a solution to final disposal methods.

Methodology

        To explore the present situation of solid waste management and its underlying problems in Dhaka, and to finally recommend a solution based on this research, a literature review was conducted on various existing waste management plans.

Site observations and pictures were also taken in three randomly selected streets in a randomly selected neighborhood in Khilgaon, Bangladesh, a division of Dhaka with a similar demographic background as the rest of the city.15

Literature Review

        Japan International Cooperation Agency’s, or JICA’s, The Study on the Solid Waste Management in Dhaka City Volume 1 & 2, published in 2005, describes existing solid waste management plans in Dhaka and highlights the several issues and irregularities with the current plan, as well as provide recommendations for a new plan.

        JICA overviews the entire primary and secondary waste collection system in Dhaka and points out problems within each collection system. It then outlines a master plan to address such issues.

Primary Collection

On the primary collection level, JICA’s study revealed that in 2005, 88% of upper-income households, 75% of middle-income households, and only 30% of lower-income households received door-to-door collection service. It showed that the legal responsibility of door-to-door service varies, where in new urban areas, CBOs are primarily responsible, however in old Dhaka, DCC cleaners are mainly equipped with the task. It also showed that 88% of residents who receive door-to-door service pay for it at relatively low rates. Finally, it showed 85% of residents are satisfied with door-to-door service.

        Public opinion of door-to-door collection: Existing primary collection in Dhaka through door-to-door collection seems adequate according to JICA, where a relatively high number of residents receive the service and are satisfied with it.

Issues with primary collection: However, the report hints at several issues within the larger frame of primary collection: 51% of lower-income residents dump their waste in vacant lands, river, or marshes due to not being provided door-to-door service or not having a street-side waste bin, or collection point, near them. This combined with significant littering leads to an overwhelming flow of uncollected solid waste produced in the city.

Response from the study: JICA recommends an organized plan between city wards, the DCC, and primary collection providers to speed the growth of door-to-door services and street-side waste bins especially in low-income areas, as seen in Figure 10.

Figure 10: Partnership of Stakeholders in Primary Collection9

The JICA report also proposes a preliminary plan to make street-side waste bins accessible to all residents, as seen in Figure 11.

Rickshaws used for transport: The design of rickshaws vans used by primary collectors to transfer waste from buildings to DCC containers, or street-side waste bins, should also be altered to avoid waste being scattered around containers and to shorten the amount of time of transfer.

Public involvement in primary collection: Finally, the JICA report expresses a dire need for raised public awareness through IEC (Information Education and Communication) activity, especially at the community level. It suggests educating the younger generation on waste management skills. The report also stresses that decision-makers such as the mayor of Dhaka and high-ranking DCC officers should be familiar with Dhaka’s solid waste management system, and should also educate staff as well as boost employee morale.

Secondary Collection

On the secondary collection level, JICA’s report revealed that 58% of residents in new urban areas and 52% of residents in old urban areas live more than 300 feet away from street-side waste bins or containers.

Public opinion on secondary collection: In contrast to primary collection, an overwhelming majority of residents are not satisfied with the DCC’s secondary collection services, with 72% of middle-income residents and 75% of lower-income residents expressing dissatisfaction with the system. Less than half of high-income residents, however, are dissatisfied. Road and drain cleaning has a dissatisfaction rate of 69%.

Complaints regarding bins: 69% of residents complained that waste is scattered around street-side bins/containers and 34% of residents complained that such bins are too far away or are not available where they live.

Shortage of truck drivers: Another issue within the DCC’s secondary collection is the shortage of truck drivers. In 2004, JIRA found that the number of truck drivers was insufficient, with 266 drivers assigned to operate 283 trucks. The number of trucks at the time, though, was sufficient to transport 1.5 times the amount of waste produced that year.

        Inefficiency of some types of trucks: Figure 7 reveals that Container Carriers (CC) were also more cost and time-efficient than their Open Truck (OT) or Trailer Truck (TT) counterparts.

Extensive repair time for trucks: JIRA reported that truck repairs are also usually slow due to the fact that a truck can only be repaired upon the mayor’s direct approval, which often takes months or years to receive. This led to half the damaged trucks in 2004 taking two years to repair.

Public Awareness

The International Journal of Waste Resources’s Household Waste Management in High-Rise Residential Building in Dhaka, Bangladesh: Users’ Perspective, by Tahmina Ahsan and Atiq Uz Zaman, analyzes public awareness of solid waste management through a randomly selected questionnaire given to 117 residents of high-rise buildings in 2013.

Understanding public awareness of solid waste management is necessary to formulate a solution to Dhaka’s waste pollution problem. Tahima Ahsan and Atiq Uz Zaman present an exceptional opportunity to explore public awareness in their study.

        Demographic: The study’s surveyed residents made up a demographic of 62% male and 38% female. The majority of residents own their home and around 82% of residents belong to a higher income group (50,000+ Tk/month), and 18% of residents belong to a middle-income group (25,000 to 50,000 Tk/month).

Waste bins in households and buildings: The study found that at the household level, all 117 flats or apartments had only one bin to dispose of waste in. Some of the residents from these flats were given recycling bins, but they were found unused or used for inappropriate facilities such as for storing laundry. Privatization

The Center for Clean Air Policies’ Success Stories in the Waste Sector summarizes the need for expanded public-private partnerships in Dhaka’s waste management sector.

Public-private partnerships are helpful in saving state spending and serves as a useful tool for the DCC to alleviate pressure on their budget and direct saved money towards other waste management projects such as landfills.

San Francisco case study: The authors of Success Stories in the Waste Sector summarize San Fransisco Mandatory Recycling and Composting Ordinance of 2009 that required all residents, businesses, and restaurants to use color-coded waste containers provided by the city’s local waste disposal company Recology. Through a partnership with Recology, local governments incentivized residents and Recology to recycle by charging rates based on the volume of non-recyclable waste residents disposed of. The greater the volume of non-recyclable waste produced, the higher the city of San Francisco charged its residents for waste disposal. Through this mutual partnership, residents benefit from lower rates after recycling, Recology benefits from increased revenue through recycling services, and the city saves money on containers and maintenance.

The authors of this study suggest the DCC undergo similar initiatives with the private sector to duplicate success stories such as the San Francisco Mandatory Recycling and Composting Ordinance of 2009.

Analysis and Recommendations

        Rd No. 9/6 only features 1 public waste bin and Rd No. 9/2 features 2. The most notable finding of the study is that of 600-700m of Dakhin Banasree Project Road, only 2 public garbage bins were found. It is unusual for market roads to contain so few public garbage bins considering most pedestrian waste is produced in such areas.

        It is not concerning that no street-side waste bins were found on Rd No. 9/2 and Rd No. 9/8, considering these roads are only two of around a dozen neighborhood streets in the area. It is, however, quite concerning that no street-side waste bin was found in Dakhin Banasree Project Road. This suggests that the Khilgoan area experiences a deficiency in the number of street-side waste bins.

A systematic approach should be undertaken to expand the number of public garbage bins in Dhaka and to take into account bin theft and lack of bin use due to lack of fines and public awareness.

        Partnership with a private company, such as a company manufacturing public garbage bins, is strongly encouraged to speed the implementation of such bins and to alleviate spending pressure on the DCC. It also encouraged to facilitate mutual incentivization between the DCC, bin manufacturing companies, and market/residential buildings, such as in San Francisco, to implement an adequate amount of public garbage bins in Dhaka.

Primary Collection

        As the study reveals, not every part of Dhaka possesses an adequate amount of street-side waste bins. As mentioned, JICA provided a model for community primary collectors to follow, as seen in Figure 23.

Figure 23: Street-side waste bin and transportation model9

This model does not specify, however, how many containers or street-side waste bins are needed per region. Primary collectors need to take into account a neighborhood’s population as well as income level to ascertain how many street-side waste bins are adequate in a certain area. Table 2 breaks down the waste generation in one day, in kg/day, of residents in each income level. Knowing the waste density will reveal to collectors how many containers are required for a certain population. It is recommended for collectors and the DCC to perform the following calculation to find this out: The Zender Environmental Research Group states that the average kilograms of regular waste in one cubic meter is around 103 kg/m3 and the average for waste without plastic bags is around 74 kg/m3. Due to Bangladesh’s plastic ban in 2002, the waste density is likely closer to 74 kg/m3.16 The maximum occupancy of a 6m3 street-side waste bin is 623 kg of regular waste and 445 kg of waste with no plastic bags. The maximum occupancy of 12m3 street-side waste bin is 1246 kg of regular waste and 890 kg of waste with no plastic bags. A high-income area with a population of 10,000 inhabitants, for example, would then produce around 5040 kg/day of waste. This would require at least six 12m3 street-side waste bins, refilled every day.

To ensure that there is an adequate number of street-side waste bins in every Dhaka community, primary collectors and the DCC should utilize this information and perform such calculations.

It is also strongly encouraged to reassess which type of vehicle is being used by primary collectors to transport waste from buildings to street-side waste bins. Rickshaw vans, if used, should be able to easily move waste into bins, to avoid waste collection on the side of street-side waste bins.

Figure 24 diagrams the primary collection recommendations.

Secondary Collection

        The hypothetical high-income neighborhood, with a population of 10,000 inhabitants, would require its six street-side waste bins to be refilled every day, otherwise, it would require twelve bins for 2-day collections, eighteen bins for 3-day collections, twenty-four bins for 4-day collection, and so on. For this reason, an organized secondary collection method is crucial. Trucks not only need to be repaired at a steady pace, but the types of trucks in use also need to be efficient time and cost-wise. The DCC also needs to consider hiring more drivers and reassessing organization within the agency to ensure drivers are collecting an accurate and adequate amount of waste.

        Utilizing public garbage bins and thereby lowering Road and Drain Cleaning budgets could direct more available funds towards purchasing more trucks and for truck repair, and for hiring drivers.

Public Awareness

Finally, the present situation of waste management awareness is not satisfactory in Dhaka’s high-rise buildings, where a majority of residents are high-income earners. Typically, waste management awareness is not as prevalent in middle and low-income areas as it is in high-income areas, suggesting that awareness in other parts of Dhaka is even lower than that found in Ahsan and Zaman’s study.

        The sampled residents did, however, express a strong desire to change waste and recycling behavior, and acknowledged the importance of waste management systems. The study found, though, that numerous barriers such as lack of affordability and availability impede residents’ ability to improve their waste-producing habits.

It is necessary to tap into residents’ willingness to responsibly practice waste management by expanding programs that spread awareness on waste management. A useful tool is to connect with a sense of pathos. For example, Dhaka residents tend to be very patriotic about their country. It is recommended that public awareness pamphlets, posters, advertisements, word-to-mouth, etcetera, incorporate a sense of patriotism to motivate residents to practice waste management.
